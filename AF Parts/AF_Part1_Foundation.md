The Core Thesis
---
> *Money decisions create life consequences.*


That is the thesis. Five words. Every piece of content on this blog exists to demonstrate, explore, or act on that idea.


Personal finance is not a hobby niche. It is the operating system of adult life. The decision to carry a car payment you can't afford doesn't just affect your bank account — it affects which jobs you can afford to leave, how much stress you carry into your relationships, whether you can handle a medical bill without going into debt, and what financial behaviors your children absorb by watching you. One money decision. A dozen life consequences.


Adulting Finance doesn't just teach money tips. It maps the chain reaction from financial choice to life outcome. That's the difference between this blog and every other personal finance site explaining budgeting with pie charts.


➤ STRATEGIC INSIGHT: The thesis is a frame, not just a tagline. Every post you write should be able to answer the question: what money decision does this cover, and what life consequence does it connect to? If you can't answer both halves, the post isn't ready.

The Merge Rule
The core thesis is also a gate. When a potential topic comes from an adjacent area — health, relationships, career, parenting, technology — it has to pass through one question before it earns a place on this blog:


> *"What does money buy or destroy in this niche?"


That question is the merge rule. It determines whether an adjacent topic genuinely belongs here or just looks adjacent on the surface.


Here is how the merge rule works in practice:


| Adjacent Topic | Merge Rule Applied | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Sleep quality | Financial stress destroys sleep. Poor sleep destroys productivity. Productivity destroys income. | PASS |
| Medical debt | Ignoring a doctor visit costs more than attending. Debt from a medical emergency can spiral for years. | PASS |
| Couples fighting | Money is the number one cause of divorce. Financial incompatibility is a relationship risk with measurable costs. | PASS |
| Job dissatisfaction | No emergency fund = trapped in jobs you hate. Runway money = negotiating power. | PASS |
| Parenting stress | Financial instability is one of the top predictors of childhood outcomes in education and wellbeing. | PASS |
| General wellness trends | No clear money outcome. Cannot identify what money buys or destroys here. | FAIL |
| Political commentary | No clear money outcome. Fails to connect to a financial decision or consequence. | FAIL |
| AI tools in general | Only passes if the AI tool directly affects money: earning, spending, protecting, or building wealth. | CONDITIONAL |


If a topic passes the merge rule, it's on-brand. If it doesn't clearly connect to a money outcome, it's off-topic regardless of how interesting, timely, or SEO-rich it might be.


➤ ACTION REQUIRED: Before pitching or writing any post in an adjacent niche, write one sentence that completes this prompt: "This topic connects to a money outcome because _____." If you can't complete the sentence with something specific, don't write the post.

ELI12 Voice — The Complete System


---
ELI12 is the single most important editorial standard on this blog. Everything else — structure, SEO, monetization — depends on it. Master this section before writing anything else.


What ELI12 Actually Means


ELI12 stands for Explain Like I'm 12. It is not a gimmick. It is a deliberate editorial philosophy that says: the best financial writing is clear enough for a smart 12-year-old to follow and precise enough that a 35-year-old CFP wouldn't cringe.


For Adulting Finance specifically, ELI12 means writing about money the way a trusted older sibling would explain it. Not a professor. Not a banker. Not a Reddit thread full of jargon. You're sitting across the kitchen table from someone who is smart, motivated, and completely new to this topic. They need you to skip the preamble and tell them what's happening, why it matters, and what to do about it.


The "12" is intentional. A 12-year-old is old enough to understand cause and effect. They get that spending more than you earn means going negative. They understand that waiting longer means getting less. They can follow a multi-step process if each step is explained clearly. But they still need analogies. They still need real-world examples. And they will immediately check out if you throw around words like "amortization schedule" or "liquidity premium" without a translation.


That's the balance. ELI12 lives right in the middle: logical enough for an adult, concrete enough for a kid. It is not dumbing down. Dumbing down removes precision. ELI12 keeps the precision and replaces the complexity.


---


*The goal is not to make the content simpler. The goal is to make the path to understanding shorter.*


---


The Six Core Voice Attributes


1. Short sentences. Varied rhythm.
Average sentence: 12–18 words. Maximum sentence: 25 words. If a sentence hits 26 words, break it. No exceptions. But don't make every sentence the same length — that creates monotony. Three short sentences followed by one slightly longer one reads better than five identical-length sentences in a row. Think of it like music. Vary the beat.


2. Short paragraphs.
Maximum paragraph length: 1–3 sentences, never more than 4 lines on mobile. Over 60% of personal finance blog traffic comes from mobile devices. A seven-line paragraph that looks fine on desktop turns into a wall of text on a phone screen. Walls of text get skipped. One-sentence paragraphs are not just allowed — they're encouraged. Use them to land a key point.


Like this.


3. Simple words. Precise meaning.
Use the simplest word that is still accurate. "Use" beats "utilize" every time. "Start" beats "commence." "About" beats "approximately." The $1 word is almost always better than the $10 word. But this is not a blanket ban on financial terminology. You teach the vocabulary. You don't avoid it. APR, FICO, HSA, Roth IRA — these terms stay. They just get a plain-language translation on first use.


4. Analogies and examples with real numbers.
Abstract explanations don't stick. Concrete examples do. Never explain a financial concept without an example. And never use round, made-up-sounding numbers. "Save $200 a month" is weaker than "save $200 a month by cutting your three subscriptions and brown-bagging lunch on Tuesdays." Specificity is not the enemy of simplicity.


5. Direct second-person address.
Write to the reader, not about them. "You" is the most important word in this voice. Not "people who are struggling with debt" — "you, right now, reading this with $8,400 in credit card debt." The reader should feel like the post was written for them specifically.


6. Every piece ends with an action.
ELI12 is not just a voice — it's a structure. Every post ends with a clear next step. Not "be more aware of your spending." Something the reader can do today: "Open a new tab and check your credit utilization right now — the number is on the back of your card or in your bank's app."

Jargon Translation: First-Use Rule


You never strip out financial vocabulary. You translate it on first use, then use it freely.


Here's how to translate jargon in-context:


> *Compound interest — that's when your interest earns interest — means your $1,000 at 5% doesn't just grow by $50 a year. By year 10, you're earning interest on interest, and the number starts to snowball.*


After that first-use explanation, you can say "compound interest" throughout the rest of the post without stopping to define it again. The reader now owns the term.


Here is a quick-reference translation table for the most common financial terms:


| Too Complex | Use This Instead |
|---|---|
| Utilization | Usage |
| Subsequently | Then / After that |
| Approximately | About |
| Accumulation | Buildup |
| Necessitate | Require / Need |
| Fundamental | Basic |
| Demonstrate | Show |
| Expenditure | Spending / Cost |
| Amortization | Payoff schedule |
| Diversification | Spreading your money |
| Liquidity | How fast you can turn it to cash |
| Asset allocation | How you split your investments |
| Principal | The original amount you borrowed |
| Compounding period | How often interest gets calculated |

Three Side-by-Side Calibration Examples


Here is the same financial concept explained three ways: Condescending (too simple, talks down), ELI12 (the target), and Academic (too complex, alienating). Use these as calibration tools every time you write.


Topic 1: Compound Interest

|---|---|

| Academic | Compound interest represents the exponential accrual of returns on an initial principal sum, wherein each successive compounding period incorporates previously accrued interest into the base calculation, resulting in a nonlinear growth trajectory that is mathematically expressed as A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt). |

What ELI12 Is NOT


A lot of writers misread "Explain Like I'm 12" as permission to get sloppy. It's the opposite.


NOT baby talk. Your reader is 20–35, often college-educated, and will spot condescension immediately. Respect their intelligence. Eliminate the complexity, not the substance.


NOT removing all financial terms. You teach the vocabulary. You don't avoid it. APR, FICO, HSA, Roth — these words stay. They just get a plain-language translation on first use.


NOT vagueness. "Save more" is not ELI12. "Save $200 a month by cutting your three subscriptions and brown-bagging lunch on Tuesdays" is ELI12. Specificity is not the enemy of simplicity.


NOT sacrificing accuracy. If a concept requires nuance, give the nuance — just explain it simply. You can explain tax brackets accurately without sounding like the IRS.


NOT writing for actual 12-year-olds. Your audience has rent, student loans, a job, maybe a kid. The examples and stakes must reflect adult life. The delivery is what simplifies, not the subject matter.


NOT avoiding math. You show the math. You walk through each step out loud. Math with explanation is ELI12. Math without explanation is academic.


NOT thin content. ELI12 content can be 2,500 words. Length and clarity are not at war. A long article can still be Grade 7. More words does not mean more complex.

The Three-Word Test


Here is the fastest quality check in the Adulting Finance system.
After writing any paragraph, stop. Read it once. Then ask:


> *"Would a smart 12-year-old understand every word here?"*


Not every concept — a 12-year-old doesn't need to understand derivatives trading. But every word. If any single word would stop a smart 12-year-old cold, you have two options:


1. Define it in-context. Add a quick plain-language translation right after the term, the way you'd explain it mid-conversation.
2. Replace it. If the word doesn't need to be there in its complex form, swap it for the simpler version.


This test takes 10 seconds. Run it on every paragraph before you finalize a post.


Three-Word Test in practice:


*Original:* "Amortization schedules front-load interest payments, meaning the initial installments of your loan are disproportionately allocated to interest accrual rather than principal reduction."


*Test result:* ❌ Fails. Multiple stops: amortization, installments, disproportionately allocated, accrual, principal reduction.


*Revised:* "Most loan payments are set up so you pay mostly interest at the start, and mostly principal at the end. Look at a mortgage amortization table — in month one, maybe $900 of your $1,100 payment goes to interest. By year 28, that flips."


---


*Original:* "Asset allocation refers to the strategic diversification of your investment portfolio across multiple asset classes to mitigate volatility and optimize risk-adjusted returns."


*Test result:* ❌ Fails. Asset allocation, diversification, asset classes, mitigate volatility, risk-adjusted returns — dictionary entry, not blog paragraph.


*Revised:* "Asset allocation means deciding how to split your investments between stocks, bonds, and cash. Stocks grow faster but drop harder. Bonds are slower but steadier. The split you choose depends on how many years you have and how many sleepless nights you can handle."


---


*Original:* "Liquidity refers to the ease with which an asset can be converted into cash without materially impacting its market value."


*Test result:* ⚠️ Borderline. Trouble spots: liquidity, materially impacting its market value.


*Revised:* "Liquidity means how fast you can turn something into cash without losing value. Your checking account? Perfectly liquid. Your house? Not liquid — it takes months to sell and you pay fees. That's why you don't keep your emergency fund in real estate."

The Tone Dial Table


The ELI12 voice never changes. It's always you, talking directly to a 26-year-old who's smart but was never taught this stuff. You're never talking down. You're never pretending to be a textbook.


But tone shifts — and it should.


There's a difference between explaining a Roth IRA conversion ladder to someone mid-career versus cracking a joke about budget apps in a listicle. Same voice. Different dial settings. Your voice is your accent. Your tone is your volume. Same person, same accent — but you whisper in libraries and yell at concerts.


Before reading the table, understand what each dimension means in practice.


Warmth — How emotionally close are you to the reader?
1 = Clinical. Facts only. Like a user manual.
5 = Best friend on the couch. You know their debt feels embarrassing. You say so.


Urgency — How much pressure is on the clock?
1 = No rush. This information will still be here tomorrow.
5 = Do this today or you're leaving money on the table.


Authority — How confident is the voice?
1 = Fellow learner. "Here's what I found..."
5 = I've done the math. Trust me. Here's the spreadsheet and the citation.


Humor — How much fun are we having?
1 = Straight-faced. Zero jokes. Serious information delivered seriously.
5 = Memes, pop culture, analogies that make you snort. Information still accurate — just wearing a costume.


Formality — How polished is the prose?
1 = Text-your-friend casual. Contractions everywhere.
5 = Published financial report. Measured language. Adulting Finance never hits a true 5 — that would break the ELI12 voice.


| Post Type | Warmth (1–5) | Urgency (1–5) | Authority (1–5) | Humor (1–5) | Formality (1–5) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pillar / Ultimate Guide | 3 | 2 | 5 | 2 | 3 |
| Listicle | 3 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 2 |
| Scenario / Decision Playbook | 4 | 4 | 5 | 2 | 3 |
| Money Dominoes Series Post | 4 | 3 | 4 | 3 | 2 |
| Real Numbers Series Post | 3 | 3 | 5 | 2 | 3 |
| First-Gen Finance Series Post | 5 | 3 | 4 | 2 | 2 |
| Product Comparison / Affiliate Review | 3 | 3 | 5 | 3 | 3 |
| AI and Money Explainer | 3 | 3 | 4 | 4 | 2 |


➤ STRATEGIC INSIGHT: Urgency is earned, not manufactured. Don't dial urgency to 5 just to get clicks. Dial it to 5 when the math actually punishes delay — like missing an employer match or letting high-interest debt compound another month.


➤ AVOID: Letting humor swallow substance. At humor 4, you're allowed to have fun — but every post still needs a clear, useful take. "This app is bad" is not useful. "This app works great until you have more than two bank accounts, at which point it becomes a nightmare" is useful and has personality.

Flesch-Kincaid Grade 7–8 Standard


---


The ELI12 voice has a measurable target. That target is a Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level of 7 or 8.
What Flesch-Kincaid Measures
The Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level formula calculates reading difficulty using two inputs: average sentence length and average number of syllables per word. Longer sentences and bigger words push the grade level up. Shorter sentences and simpler words bring it down.


It does not measure quality. A Grade 12 paragraph is not better than a Grade 7 paragraph. It just requires more cognitive effort to decode. When your reader is already stressed about money, extra cognitive effort is the last thing you want to add.


Why Grade 7–8 Is the Target


*USA Today* writes at roughly a Grade 7 level. So does the Associated Press. Research consistently shows that readers — even highly educated ones — prefer content written below their actual reading level when they're consuming it quickly, on a phone, after a long day. Your audience is reading Adulting Finance on their lunch break or in bed. Grade 7–8 is the sweet spot where comprehension is high and friction is low.


Grade 6 and below starts to feel like a children's book. Grade 9 and above starts to feel like a textbook. Stay between 7 and 8.


How to Check Your Score


➤ TOOL: Go to hemingwayapp.com. Paste your section directly into the editor. Look at the grade level in the right-hand sidebar. Your target: Grade 7 or Grade 8. If you're at Grade 9 or higher, start editing immediately.


What to Do If Your Score Is Too High


Step 1: Find the highlighted sentences. Hemingway highlights hard-to-read sentences in red and very hard ones in pink. Attack red first.
Step 2: Break long sentences. Find the conjunction — and, but, because, which, however — and split the sentence there. Two short sentences beat one long one every time.


Step 3: Swap multisyllabic words. Use the jargon translation table in the ELI12 section as a quick reference. Any word with more than four syllables needs a reason to be there. If it doesn't earn its keep, swap it.


Step 4: Re-paste and re-check. It takes two or three passes to hit Grade 7–8 on a dense financial topic. That's normal. Build it into your editing time.


➤ ACTION REQUIRED: Every post must be checked in Hemingway before it publishes. This is not optional. The grade level check is part of the pre-publish workflow.

---


Adulting Finance publishes two distinct layers of content. Both are necessary. Neither is optional. The split is 60% ELI12 content and 40% Adult Systems content.

This is the top of the funnel. ELI12 content is simple, shareable, and designed to reach the widest possible audience. It answers broad questions in plain language. It shows up for informational searches. It earns shares on social media because it makes a confusing topic feel suddenly obvious.

An ELI12 post without a natural Adult Systems follow-up is leaving conversion on the table. An Adult Systems post without ELI12 traffic driving toward it has no audience.

Topic Filter — The Three Gates


---


Not every interesting idea belongs on this blog. The topic filter keeps Adulting Finance coherent, on-brand, and focused. Every proposed topic must pass all three gates before it earns a place in the content calendar.


Gate 1: The Merge Rule Question


> *"What does money buy or destroy in this topic?"*


If you can answer this question with something specific — a real financial outcome, a measurable money consequence, a decision with dollar stakes — the topic passes Gate 1.


If the answer is vague, generic, or nonexistent, the topic fails here. It doesn't matter how trending the topic is, how much search volume exists, or how interested the writer is. If there's no clear money outcome, it's not an Adulting Finance topic.


Gate 2: The Audience Fit Test


> *"Does this serve a reader aged 18–45 who is managing real adult financial decisions?"*


The blog's audience is young adults and adults in the thick of real money decisions — rent, debt, kids, career pivots, health costs, relationship finances. A topic that passes Gate 1 but serves a totally different demographic (retirees managing estate plans, for example, or teenagers with no income) fails Gate 2.


The question is not whether the topic is good. The question is whether it's good for this audience.


Gate 3: The Evergreen Test


> *"Will this post still be useful in 18 months?"*


News commentary fails this test. Event-specific content fails this test. Most trend pieces fail this test. Evergreen content — systems, frameworks, explanations, decision tools — passes it.


The goal is a blog full of posts that compound in value over time. A post explaining the three-bucket budget system is just as useful two years after publication as it is the day it goes live. A post reacting to last month's inflation report is worthless by next quarter.


➤ ACTION REQUIRED: Before adding any topic to the content calendar, write three sentences: one completing the Merge Rule question, one confirming audience fit, and one confirming the content will hold value in 18 months. If you can't write all three sentences clearly, the topic doesn't go on the calendar.


| Gate | Question | Pass Condition | Fail Condition |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gate 1: Merge Rule | What does money buy or destroy here? | Clear, specific money outcome | Vague or no financial connection |
| Gate 2: Audience Fit | Does this serve our 18–45 adult audience? | Topic is directly relevant to their money decisions | Serves a different demographic or life stage |
| Gate 3: Evergreen | Will this still be useful in 18 months? | System, framework, explanation, decision tool | News commentary, trend reaction, event-specific |

The Content Pairs Rule


---


Every ELI12 post has a paired Adult Systems post. This is not a suggestion. It is the content architecture of the blog.


Here is how content pairs work:


The ELI12 post introduces a problem, concept, or consequence in plain language. It builds awareness and emotional connection. It answers the question someone types into Google at 11pm when they're stressed about money.


The Adult Systems post provides the execution tool for readers who are ready to act on what the ELI12 post explained. It gives the template, the framework, the step-by-step system, or the decision matrix.


Each post links to its pair. The ELI12 post links forward to the Adult Systems post as a natural next step. The Adult Systems post links back to the ELI12 post as context and foundation. Together they form a unit.

Why Adulting Finance Exists


---


Most personal finance content was built for people who already speak the language.


It assumes you know what a mutual fund is. It assumes you understand the difference between a Roth and a Traditional IRA. It assumes you grew up in a household where these things were discussed at the dinner table — where someone showed you how to build a budget, explained how credit works before you got your first card, and talked about money as a normal, manageable part of life.


A lot of people didn't get that.


They grew up in households where money was a source of stress, silence, or shame — not a skill set. They entered adulthood without a map. They got their first credit card without understanding utilization. They took on their first car payment without understanding how it would constrain every other financial decision for the next five years. They got to their late twenties with a decent income and still wondered why they always felt broke.


Adulting Finance exists for those people.


---


*This blog exists because the most important operating system of adult life — money — is the one that nobody officially teaches you how to run.*


---


The mission is not to make financial content simpler for the sake of simplicity. The mission is to remove the barriers that stop people from accessing financial knowledge they have every right to — and every need for.


Being clear is not condescending. Writing plainly is not dumbing down. Teaching with analogies and real numbers and specific examples is not treating adults like children. It is treating them with respect. It is acknowledging that the complexity of financial jargon serves the institutions that invented it — not the people trying to navigate life without a safety net.


Adulting Finance is built on a specific belief: that when financial concepts are explained clearly, with honesty and without jargon, people change their behavior. They open the savings account. They call about the medical bill. They stop carrying the balance. They start the conversation with their partner. They make the next move.


That is the point. Not pageviews. Not affiliate commissions. Not a large social following. The point is that a real person reads something here and does something differently because of it.


Every system in this master strategy — the voice, the structure, the content pillars, the monetization architecture — exists in service of that outcome.


Money decisions create life consequences. This blog makes sure people understand that, and know what to do about it.
