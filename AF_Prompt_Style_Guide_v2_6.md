─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
THE AF BLUE SERIES — Extended Palette
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The AF Blue Series is a 10-color monochromatic blue ramp organized into
three functional tiers. Each tier has a specific role.

TIER 1 — DEEP BLUES (Dark backgrounds and anchor surfaces)
Richer and more distinctly blue than the original dark backgrounds.
Use when the design should feel "ocean depth" rather than "charcoal dark."

  Deep Space Blue:  #012a4a  → Darkest — anchor backgrounds, hero panels
  Yale Blue:        #013a63  → Primary dark surface in Blue Series designs
  Yale Blue 2:      #01497c  → Secondary dark surface, card backgrounds
  Yale Blue 3:      #014f86  → Lighter dark — subheader backgrounds, chips

TIER 2 — MID BLUES (Accent colors, interactive surfaces, data elements)
Deeper and more sophisticated than Dodger Blue (#3686FF).
Use for section accents, card borders, highlighted data points, and lines.

  Rich Cerulean:    #2a6f97  → Strong accent — darker corporate blue feel
  Cerulean:         #2c7da0  → Primary accent in calm/trust designs
  Air Force Blue:   #468faf  → Secondary accent — lighter, more approachable

TIER 3 — LIGHT BLUES (Background tints, surface washes only)
Gentle blue tints for backgrounds and card surfaces.
These are surface colors only — never use as text color.

  Steel Blue:       #61a5c2  → Mid-light — visible surface color, card faces
  Sky Blue Light:   #89c2d9  → Light tint — background washes, panel fills
  Light Blue:       #a9d6e5  → Lightest — barely-there tint, near-white blue

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
AF BLUE SERIES — COMBINATION RULES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**RULE 1 — ONE DOMINANT TIER PER DESIGN:**
Every Blue Series composition has one dominant tier that establishes the character:
- Tier 1 dominant = deep blue foundation (ocean depth, prestige, authority)
- Tier 2 dominant = mid-blue character (confident, clear, corporate trust)
- Tier 3 dominant = light blue surfaces (calm, open, approachable)

**Supporting colors from adjacent tiers may be used for accents, borders, rings,
and surface details** — but only when explicitly defined in the style template.
The dominant tier sets the visual identity. Supporting tier colors serve it.

Original dark backgrounds (#0F172A, #0D1B2A, #1A1A2E) may never be mixed with
Blue Series deep blues (#012a4a through #014f86). These palettes are separate systems.
Amber (#FDA050) is always present as the warm emotional accent in every Blue Series design.

**On the worked examples in Part 10:**
The Style 11, 12, and 13 examples legitimately use colors from multiple tiers together.
This is the correct and approved approach for those styles — Tier 1 establishes the
foundation, Tier 2 provides accent rings and borders, Tier 3 provides chip surfaces
and text accents. These are not tier-mixing violations; they are approved multi-tier
compositions. Preserve all three examples exactly as written.

RULE 2 — AMBER IS ALWAYS THE EMOTIONAL ACCENT:
In any Blue Series composition, #FDA050 Energy Orange is always present
as the warm accent. It prevents blue-heavy designs from feeling cold.

RULE 3 — TEXT ON TIER 1 DEEP BLUES:
  → Headlines: #FFFFFF or #F8FAFC
  → Body copy: rgba(255,255,255,0.65)
  → Muted labels: rgba(255,255,255,0.30)
  → Key numbers and accent lines: #FDA050
  → NEVER use #3686FF on Tier 1 — the two blues fight each other

RULE 4 — TIER 2 ON LIGHT BACKGROUNDS:
On white or Tier 3 backgrounds, use Tier 2 colors for borders,
labels, and decorative elements. They read as calm authority.

RULE 5 — TIER 3 AS SURFACES ONLY:
Never use Tier 3 colors (#61a5c2, #89c2d9, #a9d6e5) as text color.
Pair Tier 3 surfaces with Tier 1 deep blue text or #222222 Black Out.

RULE 6 — NEVER MIX WITH ORIGINAL DARK BACKGROUNDS:
Do not combine Blue Series Tier 1 (#012a4a to #014f86) with original
dark backgrounds (#0F172A, #0D1B2A, #1A1A2E). They look like a mistake.

RULE 7 — BLUE AND ORANGE ARE COMPLEMENTARY:
The Blue Series pairs naturally with #FDA050 — warm amber and cool blue
are complementary colors. This pairing is the visual signature of Blue
Series designs.

## 1.2 TYPOGRAPHY RULES

HEADING FONT: Poppins Bold (weight 700) or Poppins Black (weight 900 for display only)
BODY FONT: Inter Regular (weight 400) or Inter SemiBold (weight 600) for labels

FONT SIZE SCALE — HERO BANNERS (4K canvas at 3840×2016px):
- Display number (hero element): 400–680px Poppins Black
- Main headline: 160–260px Poppins Bold
- Accent headline line: same size as main, color #FDA050
- Eyebrow label: 28–32px Inter SemiBold, ALL-CAPS, letter-spacing 0.18em
- Subline: 48–56px Inter Regular, color #6B7280
- Small data labels: 24–32px Inter Regular or SemiBold
- Bottom detail tags: 24–28px Inter Regular, color #9CA3AF

FONT SIZE SCALE — PINTEREST PINS (2000×3000px master):
- Main headline: 120–180px Poppins Bold
- Hook number or stat: 200–280px Poppins Bold or Black
- Subline: 48–60px Inter Regular
- Eyebrow: 24px Inter SemiBold, ALL-CAPS, letter-spacing 0.16em
- Body description: 40–48px Inter Regular
- Small labels/chips: 28–32px Inter Regular

CRITICAL TYPOGRAPHY RULES:
- **Headline word count: 6–10 words total across all visible headline lines. Ideal target: 5–8 words. Hard maximum: 10 words. Never exceed 10. Line 3 (amber punch): 2–4 words. Never more than 5 words on Line 3.**
- Font weight 800 or 900 is only permitted for the single dominant display element
- Every monetary amount uses tabular numerals in Poppins Bold or Inter Bold
- Eyebrow labels are ALWAYS all-caps with generous letter-spacing
- The accent/final line of every headline is ALWAYS #FDA050 Energy Orange
- Never mix more than 2 font weights in a single visual

## 1.3 WHAT IS ABSOLUTELY BANNED IN EVERY PROMPT

State these rules explicitly inside every prompt you generate:

1. NO URL or website address inside the image
2. NO CSS spec text, design annotation text, pixel measurement labels, or hex codes
3. NO cartoon illustrations or clip art
4. NO stock-finance photography clichés — see PHOTOGRAPHY RULE below
5. NO 3D rendering, beveled effects, or embossed depth
6. NO gradient fills except one ambient radial glow at maximum 8% opacity
7. NO drop shadows except 0 40px 80px rgba(0,0,0,0.10) on fintech card elements
8. NO icons inside data visualization bars
9. NO blog name, domain name, or brand name burned into the image
10. NO source attributions naming the blog — only third-party data source names
11. NO serif fonts — Poppins and Inter only
12. NO colors outside the official palette or approved color exceptions table
13. NO mixing Blue Series Tier 1 with original dark backgrounds
14. NO Tier 3 light blues used as text colors
15. NO paragraph-level content inside banner cards — each card shows a name and one key metric. Descriptions and examples belong in the article body.
16. NO shame framing that treats financial struggle as personal failure. Never use blame-first phrases such as "still broke," "can't afford," "you're doing it wrong," or "bad with money."
17. NO panic visuals or helplessness framing. Urgency is allowed only when the design also implies agency or a next step.
18. NO diagnosis-as-hook framing. ADHD, trauma, anxiety, shame, and avoidance topics must be handled as safety/recognition topics, not cheap curiosity triggers.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PHOTOGRAPHY RULE — MASTER STATEMENT
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Stock-finance clichés are always banned. Real editorial photography is permitted
and required for narrative, emotional, life-stage, and psychology posts.

ALWAYS BANNED (in every context):
- Happy people holding credit cards
- Suited professionals pointing at graphs
- Piggy banks, coins, dollar bill fans
- Generic stock poses with financial props

PERMITTED (for Style 16, emotional/psychology posts, life-stage posts, First-Gen posts):
- Real person checking a banking app on their phone
- Person writing in a budget planner at a kitchen table
- Thoughtful, grounded real-life money moment
- Warm editorial lifestyle photography
- Natural light, everyday settings, real expression — not posed, not performative

The distinction is: editorial truth vs. stock cliché. Not people vs. no people.

## 1.4 THE SEVEN LAYOUT ARCHETYPES

Every hero banner uses one of these seven structures.

ARCHETYPE A — SPLIT PANEL DARK/LIGHT:
Dark left panel (33–40% width) — one massive dominant number or symbol.
White right panel — eyebrow, headline, subline.
Vertical accent bar (6–8px, #FDA050) separates panels.
Solid bottom bar (16–20px, #3686FF) anchors full width.
Blue Series variant: replace dark panel with #012a4a or #013a63.

ARCHETYPE B — FULL DARK PRESTIGE:
Full canvas in dark navy (#0F172A, #0D1B2A, or #1A1A2E).
Left: issue number, amber rule, large headline, muted description, detail tags.
Right: one large geometric element (circle/arc) containing result number.
Faint horizontal grid at 3% opacity. Bottom bar in #FDA050.
Blue Series variant: use #013a63 as background.

ARCHETYPE C — WARM EDITORIAL:
Full canvas in #F7F4EE or #FFFFFF.
Left: eyebrow badge, massive headline with amber accent line, subline, chips.
Right: flat editorial desk scene — no cartoons, no photos of people.
Left vertical accent bar (12–16px, #D97706 or #FDA050) on far left edge.

ARCHETYPE D — SPLIT SCREEN CONTRAST:
Dark left panel — the "before" or "problem" state.
White right panel — the "after" or "solution" state.
Center divider: 6px vertical line in #FDA050.
Full-width bottom bar in #111827 with white and amber text.

ARCHETYPE E — CARD SYSTEM:
Light canvas (#F8FAFC or #FFFFFF).
Central paycheck/income card in #1E293B, amber dollar amount.
Surrounding category cards in a grid, thin #D8E6FA arrows connecting them.
Result badge in #01AC36 below center. Bottom accent bar in #1E293B.

ARCHETYPE F — BLUE DEPTH (Blue Series only):
Full canvas in Tier 1 deep blue (#012a4a or #013a63).
Left: white headline, amber accent line, muted white subline.
Right: layered circle — Tier 2 ring, Tier 3 fill at 20% opacity, amber result number.
Horizontal rule accents in #468faf (Air Force Blue).
Bottom bar: 16px solid #FDA050.
Sensation: "looking down into deep, clear water."
Use ONLY Blue Series colors + amber + white. No Dodger Blue, no green.

ARCHETYPE G — FULL LIGHT TOP-DOWN TREE:
Full canvas in #F8FAFC or #FFFFFF.
Top: compact headline zone with eyebrow, 2-line headline, and short dek.
Center: one trunk/decision/income card that branches downward.
Lower zone: 3–4 branch cards only, each with a bucket name, one descriptor, and optional allocation chip.
Outcome line or badge below the branches.
Use for named buckets, categories, or allocation frameworks. Do not use for 2-option comparisons, 5+ option roundups, or numbered step-by-step processes.

# PART 3: TITLE-TO-BANNER HEADLINE COMPRESSION

## This step runs BEFORE writing the prompt. It is not optional.

The full SEO title and H1 live in the CMS — title tag, page heading, meta description.
They are written for Google and for readers who have already clicked.
They are NEVER placed verbatim on a hero banner or Pinterest pin.

A banner headline must be understood in under one second at thumbnail size.
Full post titles are too long, too qualified, and too search-optimized to work
as visual headlines. Putting one on a banner verbatim is the most common
failure in blog visual design.

---

## THE 4-STEP COMPRESSION PROCESS

STEP 1 — STRIP THE SEO SCAFFOLDING
Remove: bracket boosters [Free Template] [2026], keyword qualifiers, em-dashes,
parenthetical phrases, "even if" clauses, "complete guide to" phrasing.
These belong in the CMS only. What remains after stripping is the core
emotional promise — that is your raw material.

STEP 2 — BUILD THE 3-LINE STRUCTURE
Every banner headline uses exactly 3 lines unless a style template explicitly defines a shorter structure. Style 18 is the approved 2-line exception because the three bucket names form the amber second line.
  Line 1: The setup — what the post is about. Dark/white text.
  Line 2: The action or result — the core promise. Dark/white text.
  Line 3: The amber punch — the emotional hook, the "so what," the payoff.
           ALWAYS #FDA050 Energy Orange. This is the line the eye goes to first.

STEP 3 — COUNT AND CUT
**Headline word count: 6–10 words total across all visible headline lines.
Ideal target: 5–8 words. Hard maximum: 10 words. Never exceed 10.
Line 3 (amber punch): 2–4 words. Never more than 5 words on Line 3.**
Short is always stronger on a banner. Every extra word costs legibility.

STEP 4 — TEST AT THUMBNAIL
Read the 3 lines aloud in under 2 seconds.
If you stumble, it is too long.
Imagine it at 300px wide. If lines wrap awkwardly, shorten them.

## THE AMBER LINE — Writing Line 3

Line 3 is the most important line on the banner.
It is the only line in color. The eye goes to it first.
**It must always be #FDA050 Energy Orange. 2–4 words. Never more than 5 words.**
It must do exactly one of these four jobs:

JOB 1 — STATE THE RESULT in the most compressed form possible:
3–4 words maximum. Name the end state the reader wants.
Examples of the pattern: "[Outcome noun]: [past tense verb]."
"[Method name]: [brief descriptor]." "[Dollar result]."

JOB 2 — DELIVER THE EMOTIONAL PAYOFF the reader came for:
Name the feeling of relief, clarity, or confidence the post delivers.
Examples of the pattern: "Finally makes sense." "No guesswork." "Real clarity."

JOB 3 — REFRAME the post's contrarian or surprising angle:
State the unexpected truth in the fewest possible words.
Examples of the pattern: "Everyone gets this wrong." "The real number."
"Not what you think."

JOB 4 — NAME THE TIME PROMISE when speed is the core value:
State when the reader gets the result.
Examples of the pattern: "One weekend." "30 days." "Start tonight."

WHAT LINE 3 MUST NEVER DO:
- Repeat information already in lines 1 or 2
- Use a question — questions belong in Style 6 full-headline format, not line 3
- Exceed 5 words — 2–4 words is the ideal length for an amber punch line
- State the obvious — "Learn how!" and "Find out more!" communicate nothing

---

## THE CRITICAL RULE

The LLM must run the 4-step compression process on every blog post title
before writing a single word of the banner prompt.

An LLM that puts the full SEO title on the banner has skipped this step.
Compression first. Prompt second. No exceptions.

# PART 4: HERO COPY SPECIFICATION

## The Complete Copy Standard for Every Hero Banner

This section defines exactly how many words, characters, and sentences belong in
each copy element of an AF hero banner. These are not guidelines — they are
the production standard. Apply them before writing any prompt.

AF hero banners should feel like clean editorial headers — not YouTube thumbnails,
not sales banners, not crowded infographics. The aesthetic reference is a premium
magazine cover: one strong idea, one strong image, restrained text.

---

## THE FIVE COPY ELEMENTS

Every AF hero banner contains exactly these elements in this order.
The first three are required. The fourth and fifth are conditional.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ELEMENT 1 — THE EYEBROW LABEL
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What it is: A short all-caps category label that tells the reader what type
of content this is before they read the headline.

Spec:
- 3–5 words maximum
- ALL-CAPS, Inter SemiBold, letter-spacing 0.18em
- Color: #3686FF Dodger Blue (or #89c2d9 Sky Blue Light on Blue Series dark BGs)
- Never more than one line

Examples of correct eyebrow labels:
"BUDGETING TUTORIAL" / "DEBT PAYOFF" / "FIRST-GEN FINANCE" / "REAL NUMBERS"
"EMERGENCY FUND" / "SPENDING PSYCHOLOGY" / "SIDE HUSTLE GUIDE"

What it must never be: the blog name, a tagline, a sentence, or a keyword string.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ELEMENT 2 — THE HEADLINE (H1)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What it is: The primary visual headline. Compressed from the SEO title using
the Part 3 process. This is NOT the full blog post H1 — it is the banner version.

Spec:
- **6–10 words total across all visible headline lines. Ideal target: 5–8 words. Hard maximum: 10 words. Never exceed 10.**
- Split across 2–3 lines for visual rhythm
- Lines 1–2: dark or white text depending on background
- Final line: ALWAYS #FDA050 Energy Orange — the emotional hook, 2–4 words, never more than 5
- Font: Poppins Bold 700 at 180–220px (hero banner 4K master)

The 3-line structure:
  Line 1: Setup — what the post is about
  Line 2: Action or result — the core promise
  Line 3: The amber punch — emotional payoff (2–4 words, never more than 5)

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ELEMENT 3 — THE SUBHEADING / DEK
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What it is: One short editorial sentence — a dek, not a second heading.
It adds context the headline couldn't carry. It reads like a magazine
subheading, not a bullet point or a second H2.

Spec:
- 1 sentence only — never two sentences
- 12–22 words
- Inter Regular 400 at 48–56px (hero banner 4K master)
- Color: #6B7280 Muted Text on light backgrounds
         rgba(255,255,255,0.65) on dark backgrounds
- No punctuation at the end except a period

Why 12–22 words:
12 words can be a fragment. 12–22 words forces a complete sentence with
enough specificity to be useful. Under 12 = vague. Over 22 = it becomes
a paragraph and the banner starts to feel cluttered.

Good dek examples (patterns — not post-specific):
"The step-by-step method that works even when money is extremely tight."
"What the math actually shows — and when the conventional advice breaks down."
"A real plan for people starting from zero, not from a spreadsheet ideal."

Bad dek examples:
"Learn more inside." — too short, too vague
"Discover how to budget, save, invest, and stop living paycheck to paycheck
while building an emergency fund and paying off debt at the same time." — too long

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ELEMENT 4 — THE ANSWER BLOCK (conditional)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What it is: A direct 40–60 word answer to the post's primary question.
It appears in answer-driven posts as a structured text block directly below
the hero image — not inside the image itself.

When to use it:
Apply the Answer Block on informational posts where the reader's primary
intent is a direct answer to a clear question. Examples:
"What is an emergency fund?" / "How much should I spend on rent?" /
"What is zero-based budgeting?"

When NOT to use it:
Skip the Answer Block on narrative posts (Story/Narrative, First-Gen Series),
consequence chain posts (Money Dominoes), and emotional psychology posts
where the reader is not searching for a factual answer.

Spec:
- 40–60 words
- One direct answer to the primary keyword query
- Self-contained — must make sense without reading the rest of the post
- Background: #F4F7FF, left border 4px solid #3686FF, CSS class .kb-answerbox
- This is an article element, not an image element — never burn it into the banner

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ELEMENT 5 — TRUST SIGNAL (optional)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What it is: A short credibility indicator that helps skeptical readers commit.
Finance readers are inherently analytical. A single well-chosen trust signal
before the headline can dramatically increase click-through on content
that makes strong claims.

When to use it:
- Post makes a specific claim backed by data or research
- Post offers a system or method that readers might question
- Post targets a skeptical audience (debt payoff, investing, credit)
- The post's eyebrow alone is not enough to establish credibility

When NOT to use it:
- Narrative/story posts (Style 16) — intimacy is more powerful than credentials
- Simple tutorial posts — the outcome chip serves the same function
- Any post where the trust signal would compete visually with the headline

Spec:
- Maximum 5 words
- Placement: near the eyebrow label or as a small pill below the dek
- Style: very small Inter Regular, muted color (#9CA3AF on light / rgba(255,255,255,0.50) on dark)
- Must not compete with the headline in size or visual weight
- Never use it as a sales badge — it signals credibility, not a deal

Examples of correct trust signals:
"47,000 readers" / "Expert reviewed" / "BLS-sourced data" /
"Updated March 2026" / "Tested on $2,400/mo income"

Examples of incorrect trust signals:
"#1 Budgeting Blog!" — promotional, not credible
"FREE GUIDE INSIDE" — CTA energy, not trust signal
"CERTIFIED FINANCIAL ADVISOR" — too formal, breaks editorial tone

This is an image element only if the style supports it. It is never burned into
banner styles that have no room for it (Style 5, Style 1, Style 11).
Most appropriate for: Style 2, Style 4, Style 7, Style 8, Style 13, Style 15.

## VISUAL ASSET TYPE — PHOTO VS GRAPHIC

The style number determines the design approach (graphic, typography, or card system).
This rule determines whether the visual element should be a photograph or a graphic.

RULE: Match the visual asset type to the post's emotional register.

USE REAL EDITORIAL PHOTOGRAPHY when:
The post is about a life situation, emotional experience, or personal money moment.
The reader arrives feeling something — anxiety, shame, confusion, hope, transition.
The correct image shows a real person in a real money moment:
  → Checking a bank app on a phone
  → Writing in a budget planner at a kitchen table
  → Looking at a laptop with a focused or concerned expression
  → A hand holding a receipt, a bill, a calculator

Posts that typically use photography:
- Story/Narrative posts (Style 16)
- Emotional/psychology posts (Pillar 5)
- Life-stage transition posts (Pillar 4 — first job, moving out, marriage, baby)
- First-Gen series posts

Photography rules — see the PHOTOGRAPHY RULE master statement in Part 1.3.
Summary: real editorial truth, everyday settings, natural light, real expression.
No stock-finance clichés. No happy people holding credit cards.

USE A DESIGNED GRAPHIC when:
The post is about a system, method, comparison, or data finding.
The reader arrives wanting to understand a framework or make a decision.
The correct visual shows the structure, the data, or the outcome — not a person.

Posts that typically use graphics:
- Tutorial posts (Styles 2, 7, 13)
- Data-led posts (Style 10)
- Comparison posts (Styles 12, 15)
- Money Dominoes posts (Style 14)
- System/allocation posts (Styles 4, 9)
- Pillar/authority posts (Style 5)

THE DEFAULT PATTERN FOR MOST AF POSTS:
H1 headline (6–10 words)
+ one-sentence dek (12–22 words)
+ 1200×630px image (real editorial photo OR clean designed graphic)
+ optional Answer Block (40–60 words) below the image

The image supports comprehension. It never pushes useful content downward.
The first visual must appear within the top 20% of the page.
Decorative hero images that exist only for aesthetics violate this rule.

## IMAGE SEO SUPPORT RULES

These rules support search visibility without turning the banner into keyword-stuffed visual clutter.

1. **Filename:** Use a descriptive, keyword-based file name such as `primary-keyword-hero.jpg` or `primary-keyword-pin.png`.
2. **Alt text:** Write a natural description of the image. Include the primary keyword only if it fits naturally. Never keyword-stuff alt text.
3. **HTML H1:** The full SEO title must remain crawlable page text. Never rely on image text as the only H1 or keyword signal.
4. **Nearby text:** The intro or answer block should carry the search intent that was compressed out of the visual headline.
5. **Pinterest SEO:** Pin title and description should preserve the primary keyword even when the visible pin headline is compressed.
6. **Compression safeguard:** Never compress the banner headline so far that the financial topic disappears.

# PART 5: THE PROMPT GENERATION FRAMEWORK

## OUTPUT MODE DEFINITIONS

Mode A = designer spec output. It may include px values, coordinates, hex codes, layout zones, and implementation-style details.
Mode B = generator-ready prompt output. It uses natural language composition, no spec notation in the composition body, and no visible annotation risk.

---


## THE TEASE RULE

A hero banner has one job: create desire to read the article.
It does not summarize the article. It does not teach the system.
It does not explain the concept. It does not list the steps.

The reader who understands everything from the banner has no reason to click.
The reader who is intrigued but not satisfied has every reason to click.

Every banner is a movie trailer, not a plot summary.
Every banner is a magazine cover, not a table of contents.

Test: after looking at the banner for 3 seconds, does the reader know enough to
feel seen and curious — but not enough to feel satisfied? If yes: publish.
If no: the banner is teaching, not teasing. Simplify until only the hook remains.

This rule overrides visual completeness. A banner that shows one idea powerfully
is always stronger than a banner that shows the whole system correctly.

## The Seven-Step Process

STEP 1 — IDENTIFY THE EMOTIONAL REGISTER:
Is this post teaching, solving, proving, or inspiring?
For Blue Series: WORRIED (Style 11) / CHOOSING (Style 12) / FOLLOWING A PLAN (Style 13)?

STEP 2 — SELECT THE STYLE:

STEP 3 — COMPRESS THE TITLE:

STEP 4 — APPLY THE HERO COPY SPEC (Part 4) + NAME THE DOMINANT ANCHOR:

Then explicitly state:

A hero banner has one dominant hook, one compressed headline, one short dek,
one supporting visual system. That is all. Not a lesson slide. Not a table of contents.

STEP 5 — CHOOSE OUTPUT MODE:

## 5D — HERO SIMPLICITY TEST

Run this test before writing any banner prompt.
If any answer is NO, resolve it before proceeding.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
QUESTION 1 — THE ONE-SECOND TEST
Can the entire message of this banner be understood in under one second?
If NO: the concept is too complex. Strip it down to one hook + one result.

QUESTION 2 — THE ANCHOR TEST
Is there one single dominant anchor — one element the eye hits first?
If NO: the composition has two or more competing focal points. Eliminate one.
Name it: DOMINANT ANCHOR = [one line]. If you cannot name it in one line, go back.

QUESTION 3 — THE TEACHING TEST
Is this banner teaching the system instead of teasing it?
Signs you are teaching:
  - More than one card with internal descriptions
  - Steps listed out inside the banner
  - Percentage breakdowns + descriptions + examples all visible at once
  - The reader can understand the full method without clicking
If YES to any of the above: move the explanatory content to the article body.
The banner shows that the system exists. The article explains how it works.

QUESTION 4 — THE FORMAT TEST
Is this content actually suited to a hero banner, or does it belong elsewhere?
A hero banner is a wide landscape image read in 1–3 seconds.
This content may belong in a different format if it:
  - Requires sequential reading (use a Pinterest pin or in-post infographic)
  - Has 3+ steps that each need a description (use an in-post supporting graphic)
  - Is better understood as a table (use a comparison table graphic in the article)
  - Needs more than 6 text elements to communicate correctly (not a banner concept)

QUESTION 5 — THE CARD DEPTH TEST
Does any card in this layout carry paragraph-level information?
If YES: apply the Card Content Ceiling (Part 1.3, Item 15). Name only. Metric only.
No descriptions. No lists. No sub-labels.

QUESTION 6 — THE ANNOTATION RISK TEST
Does this concept require any technical language to describe it in a prompt?
(px values, rgba notation, coordinates, font names, CSS terms)
If YES and you are writing Mode B: translate everything to visual language first.
Run Section 5B translation habits before writing. Run the 5C QC checklist after.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WHEN TO USE A DIFFERENT FORMAT INSTEAD OF A HERO BANNER:

If the concept fails Questions 1, 3, or 4, consider these alternatives:
- In-post supporting graphic: full-system diagram inside the article body
- Pinterest pin: vertical format, more text space, detail-tolerant
- Comparison table graphic: structured data, not a banner
- Checklist graphic: sequential steps, not a banner concept

Pillar posts, bucket-allocation posts, and system posts in particular often need
a simplified hero (one headline + one result anchor) paired with richer in-post visuals —
not a hero that attempts to show the whole system at once.

# PART 6: PINTEREST PIN ADAPTATIONS

All brand system rules apply. Vertical composition requires different hierarchy.

## 6.1 Pinterest-Specific Rules

Hook text must appear in the top 30% of the pin — the visible zone before expanding.
Every pin has three information layers in this order:
  1. Hook — what makes someone stop scrolling
  2. Promise — what they get by clicking
  3. Credibility signal — a stat, specific outcome, or trust indicator
All text must be legible at 236px wide (standard Pinterest mobile column).
No blog domain burned into the pin graphic.

## 6.2 The Three-Pin Strategy Per Post

Pin A — published on launch day. Title Focus.
The hook is the post title rewritten as a bold, benefit-driven statement.
Highest color energy — this pin needs to stop the scroll.

Pin B — published 7 days after launch. Data Focus.
The hook is the most surprising statistic from the post.
A large number anchors the top zone. Curiosity and mild alarm are the triggers.

Pin C — published 14 days after launch. Outcome Focus.
The hook is what the reader's life looks like after applying the post's advice.
Warmer, more editorial tone. Aspiration and relief are the triggers.

## 6.3 Blue Series Pinterest Notes

Style 11 (Deep Ocean Trust) creates a calm, scroll-stopping pin on Pinterest
where warm orange and red tones dominate — the blue stands out distinctly.

Style 12 (Cerulean Clarity) works especially well for Pin B (data focus)
because the bold cerulean left panel with a white number is immediately legible
at Pinterest thumbnail size.

Style 13 (Blueprint) is better suited to hero banners than Pinterest pins.
The structural detail becomes hard to read at mobile pin sizes.
Use for feature-pin content where the image displays at larger sizes only.

## 6.4 New Style Pinterest Notes

Style 17 (Fintech Card) works best for Pin B data-focus pins when the UI card is heavily simplified. Use one large result row, 2–3 broad rows or colored bands, and no tiny interface text.

Style 18 (Flow Tree) usually does not adapt well to Pinterest if the full tree is preserved. Use only as a simplified three-branch vertical stack, or move the full framework into an in-post graphic.

Style 19 (The Rule) is a strong Pinterest candidate when the rule or proportion bar is large, clean, and visually dominant. Keep labels few and oversized.

All three new styles must pass the top-30% hook test and remain readable at mobile feed size.

## 6.5 Pinterest Prompt Additions

Add these three lines to the standard template for every Pinterest prompt:

Canvas: "Canvas is 2000px wide × 3000px tall (2:3 ratio). Hook must appear in
the top 600px. Every text element must be legible at 500px wide mobile preview."

Safe zone: "Keep all text, headlines, and key visual elements well inside the canvas,
leaving generous clear margin on all four sides — nothing important should sit
close to any edge."

Scroll-stop: "This pin must communicate its core hook within 0.5 seconds of viewing."

# PART 12: WRITING STYLE TRIO TABLE

For every content calendar request, match the Writing_Style value to one of the
blocks below. Each block defines Slot 1 (primary), Slot 2 (variant), and Slot 3
(title-scan trigger → wildcard if found / fallback if not).

The compressed headline from Step 3 is shared across all 3 prompts.
Generate all 3 in order. Do not skip or merge slots.

## TUTORIAL / HOW-TO
CSV values: Tutorial · How-To Guide · How-To + Template · How-To + Checklist
            How-To + Script · How-To Roadmap · How-To + Walkthrough

SLOT 1 — Style 2: Weekend Reset
WHY IT FITS: Tutorial posts need to feel achievable before they feel instructional.
Style 2's warm cream canvas, flat desk scene, and outcome chips signal "you will complete
this" — not "here is information." The three-line headline with an amber payoff line
delivers the time promise. Use when the post walks through a process the reader will
finish in a single session. If Format = "How-To + Template" and the output is a data-heavy UI, tool, budget table, or app-like deliverable, override to Style 17.

SLOT 2 — Style 7: 48 Hours / Time Promise
WHEN IT WORKS DIFFERENTLY: Use when a specific timeframe appears in the title
(30 days, 20 minutes, one weekend). The giant time number as the dominant visual
element creates urgency that Style 2's desk scene doesn't. Style 2 = "you can do
this." Style 7 = "you can do this by Tuesday."

SLOT 3 — Title scan: look for "steps" / "phases" / "system" / "automate" / "setup"
  → WILDCARD: Style 13 Blueprint
    WHY: A tutorial that teaches a multi-step system needs the structured plan visual,
    not the warm desk scene. Blueprint signals "follow this and you'll have a working
    system" — stronger than Style 2 for anything procedural and repeatable.
  → FALLBACK: Style 4 Every Dollar Has a Job
    WHY: When the tutorial is about money allocation or category assignment,
    the card-and-arrows flow diagram is more instructional than a desk scene.

## ELI12 EXPLAINER
CSV values: ELI12 Explainer · ELI12 + System · ELI12 + Fix · ELI12 + Science
            ELI12 + Framework · ELI12 + Psychology · ELI12 + Comparison

SLOT 1 — Style 8: Magazine Cover
WHY IT FITS: ELI12 posts are definitional — "here is what X actually is." Style 8's
enormous type, warm cream canvas, and single amber accent line signal editorial authority
without complexity. The large Poppins headline IS the visual. No chart, no card system,
no split panel competing for attention. Use for "what is X" and "how does X work" posts
where clarity is the entire job.

SLOT 2 — Style 6: Missing Money
WHEN IT WORKS DIFFERENTLY: Use when the ELI12 post is anchored in anxiety — "why does
X keep happening to me?" rather than "what is X?" The dark question hook triggers the
reader's recognition before delivering the explanation. Style 8 teaches. Style 6 first
makes the reader feel understood.

SLOT 3 — Title scan: look for "%" / "ratio" / "rule" / "50/30/20" / "15%" / "28%" / "why" / "what happens" / "what nobody" / "secret"
  → WILDCARD: Style 19 The Rule when the trigger is a percentage, ratio, or named financial rule.
    WHY: If the post itself is a rule or formula, the proportion visual is clearer than a generic explainer cover.
  → SECONDARY WILDCARD: Style 1 Zero Shock when the trigger is a shocking standalone result number.
    WHY: A counterintuitive outcome number belongs as the dominant left-panel element.
  → FALLBACK: Style 8 (primary confirmed)
    WHY: No trigger found means the post is genuinely definitional. Style 8 is correct.

## STORY / NARRATIVE
CSV values: Story / Narrative · ELI12 Story · First-Gen Series

SLOT 1 — Style 16: The Real Story
WHY IT FITS: Narrative posts live or die on the sense that a real person with a real
situation is speaking. Style 16's pull quote as the dominant visual element makes the
reader's emotional recognition the entire premise of the banner. The warm cream canvas
and amber specific number (dollar amount, timeframe) are the only design signals needed.
Use for first-person accounts, case studies, and first-gen posts.

SLOT 2 — Style 8: Magazine Cover
WHEN IT WORKS DIFFERENTLY: Use when the narrative post carries editorial authority
rather than personal vulnerability — research-backed psychology posts, first-gen
finance explainers where the tone is more "here's what I know" than "here's what I
felt." Style 16 = intimacy. Style 8 = confident personal expertise.

SLOT 3 — Title scan: look for "we paid off" / "$" + number + "months" / "I went from"
  → WILDCARD: Style 1 Zero Shock (with the dollar or result number as dominant element)
    WHY: A before/after story with a specific financial outcome is stronger with the
    result number anchoring the dark left panel. The number creates the stop-scroll
    moment before the narrative delivers the story.
  → FALLBACK: Style 16 (primary confirmed)
    WHY: No specific financial result in title = the story is experiential, not
    outcome-anchored. Style 16 is correct.

## DATA-LED / REAL NUMBERS / CASE STUDY
CSV values: Data-Led · Real Numbers · Case Study · Listicle + Data

SLOT 1 — Style 10: Split Data Story
WHY IT FITS: The entire premise of a data post is "this number proves something you
didn't know." Style 10's dark left panel with one dominant statistic in white makes
that number the first thing the eye sees. The white right panel tells the story of
what the number means. This is the correct structure for any post where a research
finding or real measurement is the hook.

SLOT 2 — Style 1: Zero Shock
WHEN IT WORKS DIFFERENTLY: Use when the number is a financial result rather than a
statistic — $0 balance, debt paid off, a specific dollar amount earned. Style 10 =
"the data says X." Style 1 = "the result was X." Style 1 is more personal; Style 10
is more institutional. Both work for data posts — the distinction is whether the
number is research or outcome.

SLOT 3 — Title scan: look for "%" / "average" / "we tracked" / "we analyzed"
  → WILDCARD: Style 10 Blue variant (replace #222222 panel with #013a63 Yale Blue)
    WHY: Research-sourced statistics from institutional sources (BLS, Federal Reserve)
    feel more credible with the Blue Series institutional tone. The data came from a
    trusted source — the visual should signal that.
  → FALLBACK: Style 5 Premium Dark Cover
    WHY: No statistic trigger found means the data post is more comprehensive/authority
    than purely number-led. Pillar-adjacent data posts work better in the full-canvas
    dark prestige layout.

## MONEY DOMINOES / CONSEQUENCE CHAIN
CSV values: Money Dominoes · Consequence Chain · Real Cost / Consequence Chain

SLOT 1 — Style 14: Domino Effect
WHY IT FITS: This is the dedicated style for consequence chain posts — 4 nodes,
dark canvas, amber→red→red→green chain. It is the only style in the system that
visualizes a causal sequence. Use for every post where one financial decision
cascades into downstream life consequences. See Part 11 for the complete prompt
template and the mandatory Node 4 green rule.

SLOT 2 — Style 6: Missing Money
WHEN IT WORKS DIFFERENTLY: Use when the consequence chain is framed as a question
rather than a statement — "where does your money go when you do X?" The dark
question hook creates anxiety before the chain visual in the article delivers the
answer. Style 14 shows the chain directly. Style 6 builds the question first.

SLOT 3 — Title scan: look for "$" + dollar amount OR specific timeframe ("10 years" /
"by retirement" / "over 5 years")
  → WILDCARD: Style 1 Zero Shock (with the dollar consequence as the dominant number)
    WHY: If the title contains the end-state dollar figure, that number is more powerful
    as the dominant left-panel element than as one node in a chain. "$47,000" in 580px
    amber on dark navy stops the scroll before the reader reads a single word.
  → FALLBACK: Style 14 (primary confirmed)
    WHY: No specific dollar amount in title means the chain visual is the correct anchor.

## COMPARISON — 2 OPTIONS
CSV values: Comparison · Comparison (Affiliate) — where title contains "vs" or
            compares exactly 2 named options

FIRST — CHECK THE TITLE: Does the title contain "vs" or name exactly 2 options?
YES → Load this trio. NO → Load the Multi-Option Comparison trio below.

SLOT 1 — Style 12: Cerulean Clarity
WHY IT FITS: The bright cerulean left panel with one dominant number (the key metric
that differentiates the two options) gives the reader the most important data point
before they read a word. The two comparison chips on the right show both options at
equal visual weight — no winner pre-selected. This banner invites the click without
deciding the answer.
NO-WINNER RULE: No highlighted chip. No checkmark. No "Best Pick" badge. No "winner"
label. The banner shows data. The post decides. This rule is not optional.

SLOT 2 — Style 10: Split Data Story
WHEN IT WORKS DIFFERENTLY: Use when the comparison is driven by a surprising data
finding rather than a feature-by-feature battle. "HYSA rates are 50× higher than
traditional savings" is a data story — the stat is the banner. "HYSA vs Money Market"
is a feature comparison — two chips side by side. Data post → Style 10.
Same no-winner rule applies.

SLOT 3 — Title scan: look for "worth it" / "actually" / "myth" / "wrong" / "stop"
  → WILDCARD: Style 3 Chaos vs Control
    WHY: A comparison post with a contrarian framing — one option is clearly wrong
    or harmful — works better as a before/after split than a neutral comparison.
    The dark left = the bad choice. The white right = the right choice. This is the
    only case where a comparison banner can suggest direction.
  → FALLBACK: Style 12 (primary confirmed)
    WHY: Neutral comparison, no contrarian angle. Style 12 is correct.

## COMPARISON — MULTI-OPTION (3+ options)
CSV values: Comparison (Affiliate) — where title contains a leading number ("5 Best" /
            "Top 4" / app showdowns with 3+ named products) · Comparison Listicle ·
            Ranked Listicle

FIRST — CHECK THE TITLE: Does it have a leading number (3+) or name 3+ products?
YES → Load this trio. NO → Load the 2-Option Comparison trio above.

SLOT 1 — Style 15: The Showdown
WHY IT FITS: Multi-option comparisons need a grid — one card per option, equal visual
weight, no pre-selected winner. Style 15's neutral multi-card layout with a "which fits
your situation?" decision row at the bottom signals editorial integrity, not affiliate
optimization. Highest-affiliate-revenue posts on the site — this banner earns the click
through trust, not through pressure.
NO-WINNER RULE: Identical visual weight for every card. No highlighted border on any
single card. No "Editor's Choice" badge. No star ratings without disclosed methodology.

SLOT 2 — Style 9: Sticky Note System
WHEN IT WORKS DIFFERENTLY: Use for comparison posts that are category-based rather
than product-based — comparing 5 budgeting methods, 6 debt payoff approaches, 7 types
of savings accounts. When the options are concepts rather than branded products,
the warm card grid feels more educational and less like an affiliate roundup.

SLOT 3 — Title scan: look for "showdown" / "battle" / "which wins" / "ranked"
  → WILDCARD: Style 10 Split Data Story
    WHY: If the title signals a data-ranked comparison ("We Tested All 4"), the winning
    statistic goes on the dark left panel, and the right panel sets up the story.
    The data did the ranking — the banner shows the result.
  → FALLBACK: Style 15 (primary confirmed)
    WHY: Standard multi-option comparison. Neutral grid is correct.

## EXECUTION / SYSTEMS / ADULT SYSTEMS
CSV values: Execution / Systems · Adult Systems · How-To System

SLOT 1 — Style 13: Blueprint
WHY IT FITS: Execution posts teach a repeatable system — something the reader sets up
once and runs indefinitely. Blueprint's full blue canvas, grid texture, numbered steps,
and result card signal "this is a plan, not an explanation." Use for automation setups,
income floor systems, cashflow planners, and any post where the deliverable is a
running system, not a one-time action.

SLOT 2 — Style 4: Every Dollar Has a Job
WHEN IT WORKS DIFFERENTLY: Use when the system is specifically about money allocation
— buckets, categories, or flows from income to destinations. The card-and-arrows
diagram is the most literal representation of "here is where each dollar goes." More
visual than Blueprint for allocation frameworks. Blueprint = process. Style 4 = flow.
If the system has 3–4 named buckets/categories rather than a full money-flow diagram, use Style 18 instead.

SLOT 3 — Title scan: look for "automate" / "setup" / "never" / "runs itself"
  → WILDCARD: Style 7 48 Hours
    WHY: If the title promises the system can be set up fast ("automate in one hour,"
    "never think about it again"), the time number as the dominant visual element is
    stronger than the structured plan. The setup time is the hook.
  → FALLBACK: Style 13 (primary confirmed)
    WHY: No speed-promise in title. Blueprint is correct.

    authority with neutrality.
  → FALLBACK: Style 5 (primary confirmed)
    WHY: Standard pillar page. Dark prestige is correct.

## CONTRARIAN / MYTH-BUSTING
CSV values: Contrarian · Myth-Busting · Warning + How-To · Explainer + Warning

SLOT 1 — Style 5: Premium Dark Cover
WHY IT FITS: A contrarian post needs to feel authoritative before it feels provocative.
Style 5's restrained dark canvas with a single amber accent signals "this is a
well-researched argument, not a hot take." The evidence does the provoking; the design
signals seriousness. Use for posts that challenge conventional wisdom with data.

SLOT 2 — Style 3: Chaos vs Control
WHEN IT WORKS DIFFERENTLY: Use when the contrarian argument is a direct before/after
flip — "everyone does X (dark left) but Y is what actually works (white right)." The
split-screen makes the correction visual rather than verbal. Style 5 = "here is the
argument." Style 3 = "here is the wrong way and here is the right way."

SLOT 3 — Title scan: look for "myth" / "wrong" / "stop" / "actually" / "nobody tells"
  → WILDCARD: Style 6 Missing Money
    WHY: Strong trigger words signal a curiosity-gap post. The dark question hook
    creates the "wait — am I doing this wrong?" moment before the argument lands.
    More emotionally provocative than Style 5's authority framing.
  → FALLBACK: Style 5 (primary confirmed)
    WHY: No trigger found means the contrarian angle is evidence-based rather than
    emotionally provocative. Authority framing is correct.

## FINANCIAL PSYCHOLOGY / MINDSET
CSV values: ELI12 + Psychology · ELI12 + Science · Psychology + Tips ·
            Psychology + System · Interactive Audit

SLOT 1 — Style 16: The Real Story
WHY IT FITS: Psychology posts work because the reader recognizes themselves. Style 16's
pull quote as the dominant element creates that recognition before any explanation begins.
The warm canvas and first-person amber line ("I checked my balance with one eye closed")
is more powerful than any chart or system visual for posts about emotional money behavior.

SLOT 2 — Style 8: Magazine Cover
WHEN IT WORKS DIFFERENTLY: Use when the psychology post is backed by science or research
— behavioral economics, cognitive bias, clinical findings. Style 16 = personal recognition.
Style 8 = intellectual authority. "The science says X" posts need the editorial gravitas
of enormous type, not the intimacy of a pull quote.

SLOT 3 — Title scan: look for "ADHD" / "shame" / "anxiety" / "avoidance" / "trauma"
  → WILDCARD: Style 11 Deep Ocean Trust
    WHY: Posts about financial anxiety, shame, or avoidance need the reader to feel
    safe before they will engage. Style 11's calm deep blue carries institutional trust
    without clinical coldness. The reader's guard comes down before the content arrives.
  → RECOGNITION OPTION: Style 16 Real Story when the title is first-person or lived-experience-led.
    WHY: Personal recognition beats institutional calm when the post's promise is "this is what it felt like."
  → RESEARCH OPTION: Style 8 Magazine Cover when the title is science/research-led.
    WHY: Intellectual authority beats intimacy when the post is explaining behavioral science.
  → FALLBACK: Style 16 (primary confirmed)
    WHY: No vulnerability trigger found. Personal recognition framing is correct.
